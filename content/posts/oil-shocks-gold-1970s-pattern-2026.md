Oil is moving. Not because of a supply glut or a Saudi policy meeting — because of strikes near Iran and renewed threats to the Strait of Hormuz. Most people will find out when they pull up to a gas station and feel it in their wallet. By then, the trade is already done.

That's how it works. The signal travels upstream long before it reaches the pump. And for those who know what to look for, the chain of cause and effect is remarkably consistent — consistent enough that it has repeated twice in the last 50 years with nearly identical results for gold prices.

This article lays out that chain, the historical record, and what the current setup looks like compared to the 1970s.

---

## The Chain: From Conflict to Commodity Prices

The path from a military strike to higher gas prices isn't random. It runs through a specific sequence, and each link in that chain has a measurable, trackable footprint.

**Step one: conflict in or near the Strait of Hormuz.** The Strait of Hormuz is a 21-mile-wide passage between Iran and Oman at the mouth of the Persian Gulf. About 20% of the world's daily oil supply transits through it — roughly 20 million barrels per day. There is no practical alternative route for most of that volume. When tension rises in that corridor, the global oil market pays attention immediately.

**Step two: tanker risk increases.** Any credible threat to shipping in the region — drone strikes on vessels, naval confrontations, Iranian Revolutionary Guard activity — raises the risk profile for every tanker operator moving oil through the Gulf. Shipping companies don't absorb that risk. They transfer it.

**Step three: Lloyd's of London widens its war risk zones.** This is the step most people never hear about, and it's one of the clearest early indicators available. Lloyd's of London, the global insurance market that underwrites a significant share of maritime shipping risk, maintains a list of "Listed Areas" — regions where vessels require additional war risk coverage. When conflict escalates, Lloyd's expands those zones. When they do, premiums for vessels transiting the affected waters increase immediately.

On March 3, 2026, Reuters reported that Lloyd's had expanded its high-risk zone in response to escalating activity near the Persian Gulf, citing increased threat levels to commercial shipping. That expansion translates directly into higher operating costs for tanker operators — costs that flow through to oil prices and, ultimately, to the fuel you buy.

**Step four: oil prices rise.** Higher insurance premiums, combined with supply uncertainty, push oil futures higher. Traders price in the probability of disruption, not just actual disruption. The market moves on fear of constraint, not just constraint itself.

**Step five: everything downstream gets more expensive.** Gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, petrochemicals, plastics, fertilizers. The energy cost embedded in virtually every product in the economy ticks higher. Inflation expectations rise. And when inflation expectations rise, investors historically rotate into assets that hold value when paper currency loses purchasing power — which brings us to gold.

## The 1970s: The Pattern in the Data

The relationship between oil shocks and gold appreciation isn't a theory. It has played out with enough consistency and magnitude that it's worth examining the numbers directly.

### 1973: The OPEC Oil Embargo

In October 1973, Arab members of OPEC announced an oil embargo targeting the United States and other nations that had supported Israel in the Yom Kippur War. The impact on oil prices was immediate and severe.

Before the embargo, crude oil was priced at approximately $3 per barrel. Within months, it had risen to $12 per barrel — a fourfold increase. Gasoline lines formed across the United States. Inflation surged. The economy slowed while prices continued rising, producing the condition economists call stagflation.

Gold's response was direct and sustained. In early 1973, gold was trading around $65 per ounce, having only recently been freed from the fixed $35 price set under Bretton Woods (the U.S. had ended dollar-gold convertibility in August 1971, but gold's price discovery in the open market was still maturing). By 1975, gold had reached approximately $180 per ounce — roughly a tripling in two years.

The mechanism was straightforward: oil shock → inflation → loss of confidence in the dollar's purchasing power → demand for hard assets with intrinsic value. Gold, which cannot be printed or manufactured at will, absorbed a significant share of that demand.

### 1979: The Iranian Revolution

The second oil crisis followed a different trigger but produced a structurally identical outcome.

In January 1979, the Iranian Revolution ousted Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi and brought Ayatollah Khomeini to power. Iran, at the time one of the world's largest oil exporters, saw its production collapse. Iranian oil output dropped from roughly 6 million barrels per day before the revolution to under 3 million barrels per day in the aftermath, with periods of near-zero production during the political transition.

Oil prices roughly doubled between 1979 and 1980, from approximately $15 per barrel to over $35 per barrel. Combined with the residual inflationary pressure already present in the global economy from the 1973 shock, the effect on confidence in fiat currencies was significant.

Gold responded with one of the most dramatic moves in its modern history. Entering 1979, gold was trading around $225 per ounce. By January 1980, it had reached $850 per ounce — an increase of roughly 275% in approximately 12 months. That January 21, 1980 peak ($850) remained an all-time nominal high for gold for more than 25 years, until the metal surpassed it in 2008.

The driver, again, was the same: an energy shock of sufficient magnitude to generate genuine inflation fear, which sent capital flowing into stores of value that central banks couldn't devalue.

### The Consistent Mechanism

Two different crises, two different triggers, same outcome. The mechanism in each case:

1. Military or geopolitical disruption hits a major oil supply node
2. Oil prices spike sharply and quickly
3. Inflation expectations surge
4. Confidence in fiat currency weakens
5. Capital rotates into hard assets, particularly gold
6. Gold appreciates substantially, often outpacing oil's own percentage gain on a risk-adjusted basis

The key insight is that gold doesn't merely track oil. It tracks the *inflation expectation* that oil shocks generate. That distinction matters, because it means gold can continue rising even after oil stabilizes — as long as inflation fear persists. In both the 1973 and 1979 cases, gold's appreciation extended well beyond the initial oil shock itself.

## 2026: Reading the Current Setup

The current situation shares structural features with both 1973 and 1979, though no two geopolitical moments are identical.

**Oil is moving on Iran conflict.** Strikes targeting Iranian assets and the resulting retaliatory threats to Gulf shipping have introduced genuine uncertainty into the supply chain. The Strait of Hormuz remains the focal point. Even a partial disruption — not full closure, but enough to slow traffic and raise risk premiums — would affect global supply with measurable speed.

**The Lloyd's signal has already fired.** As noted, the March 3, 2026 Reuters report on Lloyd's expanding its high-risk zone is not a prediction of what might happen. It's a real-time indicator that institutional underwriters — who price risk for a living and whose financial exposure requires them to be accurate — have already reassessed the threat environment. Insurance markets are not emotional. When they move, it's because the underlying risk has moved.

**Gold is already near all-time highs.** This is important context. Unlike 1973, when gold was still finding its market price after decades of fixed exchange rates, gold in 2026 is entering this period at elevated levels. That cuts both ways: the upside move may be smaller on a percentage basis than 1979's, but the market is also not starting from a depressed base. Gold at current levels reflects that institutional and sovereign buyers are already treating it as a reserve asset in an uncertain inflation environment.

**The 20% Hormuz figure is not theoretical.** One-fifth of the world's daily oil supply transits through a chokepoint that is currently under elevated threat. There is no spare capacity equivalent elsewhere that can be activated quickly enough to offset a significant disruption. Saudi Arabia and the UAE together hold the world's largest cushion of spare production capacity, but that capacity produces crude that still has to move through or near the same contested waters before it reaches global markets. This is a structural vulnerability in the global energy system, and it is the same vulnerability that made the 1973 and 1979 shocks so consequential.

**The broader macro backdrop amplifies the signal.** In the 1970s, the United States was dealing with residual inflation from the Vietnam War spending era and the collapse of the Bretton Woods dollar peg. In 2026, global debt levels are at historic highs, central banks have less room to maneuver than they did entering the 1970s tightening cycle, and monetary credibility has been tested by the post-2020 inflation episode. An energy shock hitting a system already sensitized to inflation is not the same as an energy shock hitting a stable low-inflation environment. The kindling is already dry.

## What Informed Investors Watch

The gas station is a lagging indicator. By the time prices at the pump reflect what's happening in the Gulf, the moves in oil futures, shipping insurance, and gold have already been underway for days or weeks.

Here's where the information actually lives, earlier in the chain:

**War risk premiums from Lloyd's and comparable maritime insurers.** These move in real time and reflect the actual risk assessment of professionals with financial skin in the game. A widening of the listed high-risk zone is an operational signal, not a media narrative.

**Crude oil futures, particularly the front-month contract.** The futures market prices in expected supply and demand before physical barrels change hands. When geopolitical risk increases, the futures curve shifts. Watching the spread between near-term and longer-dated contracts (backwardation vs. contango) gives information about how severe traders believe the disruption will be and how long they expect it to last.

**Gold's behavior relative to real interest rates.** Gold tends to perform best when real interest rates (nominal rates minus inflation) are low or negative — which is exactly the environment that an energy-driven inflation spike creates. If oil rises and the Federal Reserve is constrained in its ability to raise rates aggressively (as it was in the late 1970s), real rates stay compressed and gold has room to run.

**The dollar index.** Oil shocks that generate dollar weakness amplify gold's move, since gold is priced in dollars. A weakening dollar makes gold cheaper for foreign buyers, increasing demand and pushing prices higher in USD terms simultaneously.

The framework is simple: follow the chain upstream. Conflict → tanker risk → insurance premiums → futures pricing → physical oil prices → inflation expectations → gold. Each link is observable before the next one fully manifests. The people who wait for pump prices are reacting to the last link in a chain that was already forming weeks earlier.

## Conclusion

The 1970s established a repeatable pattern: military disruption in or near major oil supply nodes generates energy price shocks, which generate inflation fear, which generates demand for assets that hold value when currencies don't. Gold has been the primary beneficiary of that dynamic twice in the last 50 years.

The setup in early 2026 — Iran conflict, Strait of Hormuz risk, Lloyd's expanding its war risk zones, oil already moving — is structurally familiar. Whether it produces an outcome of the same magnitude as 1979 depends on factors that haven't resolved yet: the duration of the conflict, the Fed's response to inflation, the degree of actual supply disruption, and how much of this is already priced into the current gold level.

What's not in question is that the chain is forming. The signals are visible for anyone watching upstream. The gas station will tell you about it eventually. The question is whether you're already positioned by then.
